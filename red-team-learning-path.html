LEARNER QUALIFICATION

Who This Learning Path Is Designed For

This learning path is intended for practitioners who want to develop Red Team skills through structured, hands-on lab execution rather than passive learning.

Designed For
  • Aspiring Red Team practitioners — Learners building offensive skills through guided lab execution.
  • Security professionals transitioning roles — Blue teamers, analysts, or pentesters moving into Red Team work.
  • Certification candidates — Learners seeking deeper execution practice alongside assessments.
  • Self-directed learners — Individuals comfortable progressing independently through complex environments.
Not Designed For

  • Shortcut seekers — Progression is deliberate and cannot be rushed.

LAB-FIRST APPROACH

How Learning Happens in the Labs

The learning path is designed around execution-first practice, where understanding is built through direct interaction with realistic environments rather than isolated theory.

Execution before explanation

Learners engage with the environment first, then refine understanding through iteration and reflection.

Failure as a signal

Labs are designed to allow mistakes, encouraging analysis, correction, and deeper comprehension.

Progressive complexity

Each stage introduces new constraints, dependencies, and operational considerations.

Context over tools

The focus is on understanding attack paths and decision-making, not memorizing individual tools or commands.

Repetition with variation

Similar concepts reappear across labs in different contexts to reinforce transferable skills.

This approach mirrors how real Red Team skills are developed in practice—through repeated execution, adjustment, and contextual learning rather than linear instruction.

PROGRESSION MODEL

How the Learning Path Is Structured

The learning path is organized into progressive stages, each designed to build specific capabilities before introducing additional complexity.

Foundation Labs
Focus: Environment understanding and access fundamentals

Learners begin by working within controlled enterprise-style environments to understand identity structures, system relationships, and common misconfigurations.

Outcome: Ability to interpret enterprise environments and establish initial access with intent.
Intermediate Labs
Focus: Movement, escalation, and persistence

Labs introduce lateral movement, privilege escalation, and interaction across multiple systems, requiring deliberate planning and execution.

Outcome: Ability to move through environments intentionally and maintain access across systems.
Advanced Labs
Focus: Enterprise-scale attack paths

Complex environments introduce forest-level structures, trust relationships, and operational constraints that reflect real Red Team engagements.

Outcome: Ability to plan and execute complete attack paths across enterprise environments.

Each stage assumes completion of the previous one. Progression is designed to be sequential, ensuring skills are developed in context rather than accumulated randomly.

FOUNDATION LABS

Building Core Understanding of Enterprise Environments

Foundation labs establish the baseline skills required to understand how enterprise environments are structured and where attack paths begin.

What You Work On
  • Enterprise identity fundamentals — Understand how users, groups, and permissions are structured and misconfigured in real environments.
  • Environment enumeration — Learn to observe and map systems, relationships, and trust boundaries before taking action.
  • Initial access techniques — Practice gaining access through common weaknesses without relying on automation or shortcuts.
  • Situational awareness — Develop the ability to interpret what matters in an environment and what does not.
How the Labs Are Designed
  • Controlled environments — Labs focus on clarity and learning signal rather than scale or noise.
  • Single-domain focus — Environments are intentionally scoped to reduce complexity while reinforcing fundamentals.
  • Repeatable execution — Key concepts are practiced multiple times across different scenarios to build confidence.
Capability Outcome: After completing the Foundation stage, learners are able to analyze enterprise-style environments, identify meaningful weaknesses, and establish access with intent rather than trial and error.
OPERATIONAL MOVEMENT

Expanding Access and Control Across Systems

Intermediate labs focus on operating across multiple systems, requiring deliberate movement, escalation, and persistence rather than isolated actions.

Learning Focus
  • Lateral movement — Learn to move between systems by leveraging credentials, trust relationships, and environmental weaknesses.
  • Privilege escalation — Practice identifying and exploiting paths to higher privilege with intent and control.
  • Persistence strategies — Establish and maintain access while adapting to environmental changes.
  • Multi-system interaction — Coordinate actions across hosts, services, and identities without breaking operational flow.
Environment Characteristics
  • Increased interdependency — Actions in one system affect others, requiring planning and foresight.
  • Reduced guidance — Labs provide fewer hints, encouraging independent analysis and decision-making.
  • Operational constraints — Scenarios introduce limitations that mirror real-world conditions.
Capability Outcome: After completing the Intermediate stage, learners can traverse enterprise environments methodically, maintain access across systems, and adapt their approach based on evolving conditions.
ENTERPRISE-SCALE OPERATIONS

Executing Complete Attack Paths in Complex Environments

Advanced labs simulate enterprise-scale environments where learners must plan, execute, and adapt full attack paths under realistic operational constraints.

What Changes at This Stage
  • Forest-level environments — Labs introduce multiple domains, trust relationships, and enterprise-wide dependencies.
  • Attack path planning — Learners must evaluate multiple options and select viable paths based on context and constraints.
  • Defense-aware execution — Scenarios require awareness of visibility, logging, and operational risk.
  • End-to-end operations — Activities span initial access through impact, requiring continuity and discipline.
How Advanced Labs Are Experienced
  • Minimal guidance — Learners are expected to reason independently and validate assumptions through execution.
  • Increased realism — Environments reflect how modern enterprises are structured and monitored.
  • Higher consequence actions — Mistakes have cascading effects, reinforcing careful decision-making.
Capability Outcome: After completing the Advanced stage, learners are able to design and execute complete Red Team operations across enterprise environments, adapting to complexity, constraints, and defensive realities.
ENVIRONMENT PROGRESSION

How Lab Environments Evolve Across Stages

Lab environments are designed to evolve alongside skill progression, increasing in realism, dependency, and operational constraint as learners advance.

How Environments Change
  • Scope expansion — Environments grow from focused, single-domain setups to multi-domain enterprise structures with complex dependencies.
  • Increased realism — Later labs reflect how modern enterprises are actually configured, including legacy systems, misalignments, and operational debt.
  • Richer context — Learners encounter more signals, relationships, and decision points rather than isolated vulnerabilities.
  • Operational constraints — Visibility, logging, and environmental side effects become part of the learning experience.
What This Enables
  • Contextual decision-making — Learners must evaluate options based on environment state, not predefined paths.
  • Attack path reasoning — Skills shift from executing steps to designing and validating end-to-end operations.
  • Transferable understanding — Concepts learned in labs map directly to real enterprise environments.

Environment progression is intentional. Each stage prepares learners for the structural and operational complexity introduced in the next.

What You Are Able to Do at Each Stage

Each stage of the learning path is designed around concrete capabilities, ensuring progression is measured by what you can execute, not what you have completed.

Capability Progression

  • Intermediate readiness — Move laterally across systems, escalate privileges deliberately, and maintain access while adapting to changing conditions.
  • Advanced readiness — Design and execute complete attack paths across enterprise environments, accounting for complexity and defensive visibility.
Practical Skill Development
  • Operational reasoning — Make decisions based on environment state rather than predefined steps.
  • Attack path design — Connect individual actions into cohesive, end-to-end operations.
  • Adaptability — Adjust techniques and approaches as constraints and signals change.

Progression through the learning path reflects increasing operational responsibility, not just technical exposure.

LEARNING CONTEXT

How Labs Connect to Certifications and Instructor-Led Training

Labs function as the execution layer within a broader learning ecosystem, supporting certifications and guided training without replacing either.

How Labs Support Certifications
  • Skill reinforcement — Labs provide hands-on execution that strengthens practical understanding behind assessment objectives.
  • Context building — Learners practice techniques in realistic environments rather than isolated scenarios.
  • Readiness validation — Progress through labs helps identify strengths and gaps before formal evaluation.
How Labs Relate to Instructor-Led Training
  • Before training — Labs establish foundational familiarity, allowing learners to engage more effectively during guided sessions.
  • Alongside training — Labs serve as an execution environment to apply concepts introduced during instruction.
  • After training — Labs enable continued practice to reinforce and extend learned techniques.

Labs are not a substitute for guided instruction or formal assessment. They provide the execution context where skills are developed, tested, and refined over time.

ACCESS & SETUP

Access, Tooling, and Lab Environments

Practical details below outline how labs are accessed, what tooling is required, and how environments behave during execution.

Lab Access
  • Self-paced availability — Labs are accessible on demand, allowing learners to start, pause, and resume based on their schedule.
  • Session persistence — Progress within a lab can be preserved to support iterative learning and retries.
  • Isolated environments — Each lab runs in a dedicated environment to prevent cross-interference and ensure consistency.
Tooling Requirements
  • Standard operating systems — Labs assume familiarity with commonly used operating systems in offensive security work.
  • Common Red Team tools — Tool requirements are practical and documented per lab, avoiding unnecessary dependencies.
  • Local setup responsibility — Learners are expected to manage their own tooling and configurations.
Environment Behavior
  • Realistic constraints — Environments include dependencies, misconfigurations, and limitations found in real enterprises.
  • Stateful interactions — Actions taken within a lab can affect subsequent steps, reinforcing careful execution.
  • Repeatable scenarios — Labs can be revisited to reinforce understanding and test alternative approaches.

Reviewing access requirements and tooling expectations before starting helps ensure a smoother learning experience and minimizes setup friction.

PRACTICAL DETAILS

Common Questions About the Learning Path

These questions address practical considerations about lab access, progression, and usage to help you plan your learning effectively.

Do I need prior Red Team experience to start?
Basic familiarity with operating systems, networking, and security concepts is expected. The Foundation stage is designed to establish Red Team–specific context.
Can labs be completed at my own pace?
Yes. Labs are self-paced and can be revisited as needed to reinforce understanding and execution.
Are labs guided step-by-step?
Labs provide objectives and context, not full walkthroughs. Learners are expected to analyze, experiment, and iterate.
Do I need specific tools or software?
Common offensive security tools and standard operating systems are required. Tool requirements are listed with each lab.
Can I skip stages and start at a higher level?
Stages are designed to be sequential. Skipping foundational stages may result in gaps that affect later progress.
How is progress measured within labs?
Progress is evaluated through task completion, environment interaction, and demonstrated capability rather than time spent.

Lab expectations increase at each stage. Reviewing prerequisites before starting helps ensure a productive learning experience.
